# Freelance GC: A New Path for Experienced Lawyers in India

For a generation of Indian lawyers, the career map was linear — law school, a few years at a firm, a senior associate position, partnership, or a move in-house. The partnership track was the aspirational endpoint. The in-house route was stable but often plateaued. What very few considered was a third path — stepping out of both tracks to serve multiple clients as a fractional General Counsel, charging a fixed retainer for a defined scope, building a book of five or six long-tenure clients, and keeping the intellectual satisfaction of in-house work without being married to a single employer.

In 2026, the fractional GC model is suddenly a serious option in India. Indian startups, mid-market SaaS companies, D2C brands, and growth-stage NBFCs increasingly need board-level legal guidance but cannot justify the cost of a full-time INR 1.5-3 crore GC. A 10- to 15-year qualified lawyer offering 3-5 days a month of GC-grade attention for a defined retainer is the exact fit. The numbers work for both sides — the client gets senior counsel at 20-30% of the full-time cost, the fractional GC earns more than a single employer would pay while retaining professional independence.

This guide walks through the fractional GC model as it is emerging in India — the legal and regulatory framework, client selection, pricing, tech stack, scope definition, and the common mistakes first-time fractional GCs make. If you have 8-20 years of post-qualification experience and are actively considering an alternative to the partnership or in-house grind, this is a practical map.

<KeyTakeaway>
- A fractional General Counsel is a senior Indian advocate who serves as outside GC for multiple growth-stage companies on a defined retainer basis, typically 3-8 days a month per client.
- The model is fully compatible with BCI Rules provided fees are retainer-based (not contingent), and the advocate maintains independence and avoids fee-sharing with non-advocates under Rule 2 of Part VI, Chapter III.
- Typical Indian fractional GC pricing in 2026 ranges INR 1.5-6 lakh per month per client, scaling with company stage and scope.
- Five to seven concurrent retainer clients is the sustainable book size; beyond that, service quality degrades and the value proposition erodes.
- The tech stack must support multi-client matter separation, secure data handling under the DPDP Act, 2023, and clear conflict-check across clients.
</KeyTakeaway>

## Why the Fractional GC Model Is Emerging in India

Three market forces are creating demand.

**Mid-market companies cannot afford a full-time GC.** A seasoned Indian GC commands INR 1.5-3 crore in total compensation in 2026. Series A and Series B companies, profitable bootstrapped businesses, and asset-light service firms simply cannot justify that spend. A fractional GC at INR 2.5-4 lakh a month solves the problem.

**Traditional external counsel is wrongly shaped for the need.** Law firms bill transactionally — they solve defined problems. A growth-stage company needs a consistent senior thinking partner across contracts, HR issues, fundraise diligence, compliance reviews and board matters. That is a GC job, not a law firm engagement.

**Supply is increasing.** Senior lawyers leaving full-time in-house roles, mid-career litigators pivoting to advisory, and tier-1 firm partners seeking alternative career structures — all three pools are producing supply of qualified fractional GC talent.

The result is a market that is young but real, with probably 300-500 fractional GCs in India today and rapid growth expected over the next 3-5 years.

## Who the Fractional GC Model Fits

Not every lawyer suits the model. The best-fit profile typically has:

- 8-20 years of post-qualification experience.
- Prior in-house experience is ideal but not essential; equivalent exposure via law firm corporate practice works.
- Strong transactional skills (contracts, equity rounds, commercial disputes, regulatory advisory).
- Strong commercial judgment — the ability to say "this is technically legal but commercially stupid".
- Comfort managing ambiguity and serving as a single point of contact across functions.
- Willingness to say no — to bad clients, to scope creep, to matters outside competence.

Young lawyers under five years of experience typically struggle — clients expect GC-grade judgement, not briefed-down advice.

## Legal and Regulatory Framework

### Structure

A fractional GC practice in India almost always operates as:

- **Individual practice** — simplest, most common.
- **LLP of advocates** — if two or more fractional GCs team up.
- **Partnership firm of advocates** — less common.

A private limited company is not permitted under Section 29 of the Advocates Act, 1961 and related BCI rules. A fractional GC cannot run the practice as a Pvt Ltd "consulting" company that also provides legal advice — that would be a disciplinary issue.

### Fee Structure Compliance

- Retainer fees are permitted and standard. Must be in writing in an engagement letter.
- Contingent fees (tied to case outcomes or deal closure as a percentage) are prohibited under BCI Rule 20 of Part VI, Chapter II.
- Performance bonuses tied to non-outcome metrics (for example, defined project completion) are in a grey zone; consult carefully before adopting.
- Fee sharing with non-advocates is prohibited under Rule 2 of Part VI, Chapter III.

### Independence Under BCI Rule 22

The advocate must maintain professional independence. In a fractional GC role, you are not an employee — you are independent counsel serving the client. Avoid structures that could recharacterise you as an employee, such as mandatory employment-like work hours, exclusive engagement, or inclusion in the client's org chart as a full-time role.

<Callout variant="info" title="Independence vs Integration — The Balance">
Fractional GC work inherently involves deep integration into the client's day-to-day — you attend management meetings, review their contracts, join board calls. This is normal and professionally permitted. The line is drawn at exclusivity and control. You must remain free to serve other clients, must control how you perform the work, and must not be subject to the client's employment-like disciplinary framework. Document the independent-contractor relationship clearly in every engagement letter.
</Callout>

## Pricing Models

Three models dominate.

### Model 1: Monthly Fixed Retainer with Hours Cap

Most common in India. Structure:

- Monthly fixed fee.
- Included hours cap (for example, 25 hours a month).
- Overage rate for hours beyond the cap.
- Unused hours either lapse or carry forward for one month.

Typical 2026 pricing:

| Client Stage | Monthly Retainer | Typical Hours | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Early-stage startup (pre-Series A) | INR 50,000 - 1,00,000 | 8-12 hours | Often preferred with equity warrants, which BCI rules make tricky |
| Series A to Series B | INR 1,50,000 - 2,50,000 | 15-25 hours | Board meetings, contracts, HR |
| Series C and beyond | INR 3,00,000 - 5,00,000 | 25-40 hours | More complex regulatory, M&A work |
| Profitable mid-market (INR 50-200 cr revenue) | INR 2,00,000 - 4,00,000 | 20-30 hours | Compliance-heavy, often longer tenure |

### Model 2: Day-Based Retainer

Less common but clean for clients with predictable needs. "3 days a month at INR 75,000 per day". Good for specific verticals like real estate projects or compliance-heavy clients.

### Model 3: Quarterly Project Retainer

For companies with lumpy work — one quarter heavy (fundraise close), next quarter light. Quote quarterly scope with a floor and a ceiling. Typically used with later-stage clients that have mature in-house finance functions but are building in-house legal.

### Out-of-Scope Matters

Every retainer must define what is out of scope. Typical exclusions:

- Litigation requiring court appearances
- M&A transactions above a threshold value
- Cross-border matters requiring foreign-law advice
- Criminal defence matters

These are priced separately when they arise, either at hourly rates or fixed-fee on the matter.

## The Engagement Letter for a Fractional GC

A fractional GC engagement letter is longer and more specific than a typical matter engagement letter. Key sections:

### 1. Scope of Services

Define what is included and what is excluded. Examples of standard fractional GC scope:

- Contract review and drafting (MSAs, NDAs, employment contracts, vendor agreements).
- General legal advisory to founders and senior leadership.
- Regulatory compliance check-ins (DPDP, GST, FEMA, SEBI where applicable).
- Board meeting attendance on request, up to X meetings a quarter.
- Support on one fundraise a year up to Series B size.
- HR / employment law advisory.

### 2. Time Commitment and Availability

- Responsiveness: email within 24 hours on business days; urgent matters via phone.
- Monthly availability commitment (days or hours).
- Scheduled monthly review call (e.g., last Wednesday of the month).

### 3. Fee Structure

- Monthly retainer amount.
- Payment terms — typically advance monthly payable by the 5th.
- GST treatment — reverse charge if client is a business entity.
- TDS — 10% under Section 194J of the Income Tax Act, 1961.
- Revision schedule — typically annual.

### 4. Independence and Conflict

- Advocate's right to serve other clients.
- Advocate's conflict-of-interest check process.
- Client's right to flag a conflict when it arises.

### 5. Confidentiality

- Mutual non-disclosure of confidential information.
- Carve-outs for legally required disclosures and professional consultations.
- Survives termination by 3-5 years.

### 6. Termination

- Either party may terminate on 30-60 days' written notice.
- Immediate termination for cause.
- Handover obligations — return of confidential materials, orderly transition to successor counsel.

### 7. Liability

- Cap on liability, typically three to twelve times the monthly retainer.
- Exclusion of consequential and indirect damages.

### 8. Dispute Resolution

- Arbitration under the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, single arbitrator.
- Jurisdiction seat (typically Bengaluru, Mumbai or Delhi depending on client base).

## Finding Your First Three Clients

The fractional GC market is largely referral-driven. Three channels produce most first clients:

**1. Founder and CFO networks.** Your former law firm clients and past employers' leadership teams are warm leads. Reach out with a thoughtful explanation of the fractional GC concept, not a pitch.

**2. Venture capital portfolio companies.** VCs often have portfolios of 30-80 companies, many of which are stuck between "use law firm" and "hire full-time GC". A fractional GC solves the gap. Build relationships with 5-10 partners at mid-stage VC funds.

**3. Substantive content and speaking.** Long-form analysis on CFO concerns (ESOP taxation, VCOC compliance, FEMA on capital rounds, DPDP for SaaS companies) positions you as a fractional-GC candidate, not as an advocate chasing matters.

Avoid aggregator platforms that market lawyers on commission — they violate Rule 2 of Part VI, Chapter III and typically attract lower-quality clients.

## Building the Tech Stack

A fractional GC needs a slightly more structured tech stack than a solo litigator, because you are juggling multiple client domains simultaneously.

### Client Separation

- Separate folder structure per client in secure cloud storage.
- Separate email threads — never mix clients in a single thread.
- Colour-coded calendar per client for time-tracking and focus.
- Conflict-check log updated before every new matter.

### Contract Review at Scale

A fractional GC typically reviews 30-80 contracts a month across all clients. AI contract review tools that understand Indian law become a meaningful productivity multiplier here — reducing average review time from 45 minutes to 15 minutes per standard MSA.

### Time and Scope Tracking

Even for fixed-fee retainers, track hours per client per month. You need to know which clients are profitable and which are eating your time. A simple Google Sheet works; a purpose-built time tracker is cleaner.

### Secure Communication

Signal or other end-to-end encrypted messenger for sensitive conversations with founders. Ensure clients understand that critical instructions should also be mirrored to email for record purposes.

## The Book Size Sweet Spot

A fractional GC typically operates a book of four to seven concurrent clients. The economics look like this:

| Book Size | Monthly Revenue | Monthly Hours Committed | Remaining Capacity | Quality of Service |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 3 clients | INR 6-9 lakh | 60-75 hrs | High | Excellent |
| 5 clients | INR 10-15 lakh | 100-125 hrs | Moderate | Strong |
| 7 clients | INR 14-21 lakh | 140-175 hrs | Low | Stretched |
| 9 clients | INR 18-27 lakh | 180-225 hrs | None | Compromised |

Beyond seven clients, the fractional GC value proposition (senior attention, deep context) starts to erode. Most successful Indian fractional GCs settle at five to six concurrent clients with careful client selection.

The right response is to decline and recommend they hire full-time. Accepting sets both sides up to fail.

## Tax and Financial Structure for a Fractional GC

Revenue from fractional GC work is professional income under the head "Profits and Gains of Business or Profession".

- **Section 44AA** mandates maintenance of books of account.
- **Section 44AB** triggers tax audit once gross receipts cross INR 50 lakh.
- **Section 44ADA** presumptive taxation is not typically attractive at the fractional GC income level because actual expenses are low.
- **TDS under Section 194J** is deducted by corporate clients at 10%.
- **Advance tax** due 15 June, 15 September, 15 December, 15 March.
- **GST** is under reverse charge when services are to a business entity; voluntary registration rarely useful below INR 50 lakh.

Keep clean monthly books. At fractional GC income levels, a monthly chartered accountant engagement (INR 8,000-15,000 a month) is an investment, not a cost.

## A 12-Month Plan to Build a Fractional GC Practice

**Months 1-2.** Define your niche and target client profile. Set up practice infrastructure (website, engagement letter, tech stack). Quit current role or wind down law firm transition.

**Months 3-4.** Activate network. Meet 20-30 relevant CFOs, founders, CSes and VC partners. Publish two substantive LinkedIn articles on a niche topic. Sign first client.

**Months 5-7.** Sign two more clients to reach a book of three. Iterate engagement letter based on first client learnings. Set up monthly review cadence.

**Months 8-10.** Scale to five clients. Raise rates on new engagements by 15%. Hire a part-time paralegal if overflow is structural.

**Months 11-12.** Rebalance — let go of any misfit client. Finalise book size and rate card for year two. Review financial performance; take stock.

## Frequently Asked Questions

<FAQItem question="Is the fractional General Counsel model legally compliant under BCI rules in India?">
Yes, provided the engagement is structured as a professional retainer with an advocate-in-charge relationship, fees are not contingent on outcomes, and the advocate maintains independence. Specifically, it must comply with BCI Rule 20 of Part VI, Chapter II (no contingent fees) and Rule 2 of Part VI, Chapter III (no fee sharing with non-advocates). The engagement letter should clearly establish the independent-counsel relationship, not an employment relationship. Avoid structures that include equity or profit-sharing in the client as part of the fee.
</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="Can a fractional GC take equity as part of the compensation from a startup client?">
Equity compensation for legal services raises multiple issues. First, BCI Rule 20 of Part VI, Chapter II prohibits contingent fees tied to litigation outcomes; equity tied to specific case outcomes is a clear violation. Second, even non-contingent equity received as partial payment for services creates ongoing conflicts — every subsequent advice to the company is advice that affects the advocate's own shareholding. Third, liquidation preferences, ROFRs and drag rights in standard startup equity structures create professional-independence concerns under Rule 22. Most experienced fractional GCs avoid equity compensation and stick to cash retainers.
</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="What is a realistic monthly retainer for a fractional GC in India in 2026?">
Monthly retainers typically range INR 1.5-6 lakh per client depending on company stage, scope, and the advocate's experience. Early-stage startups (pre-Series A) pay INR 50,000-1,00,000 for lighter scope; Series A to B companies pay INR 1.5-2.5 lakh for standard GC scope; Series C and mature mid-market companies pay INR 3-5 lakh for comprehensive coverage including regulatory work. Rates scale with complexity, predictability of demand and the advocate's domain expertise.
</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="How do fractional GCs handle conflicts of interest across multiple clients?">
Run a conflict check before every new engagement and before every new matter within an existing engagement. Maintain a written conflicts register listing each current client, key affiliates, adverse parties in current matters, and industry segments served. BCI Rule 33 of Part VI, Chapter II prohibits acting for both sides where interests conflict. In practice, fractional GCs typically avoid having two direct competitors in the same book, segregate knowledge strictly across clients, and use waiver letters for non-material, non-adverse overlaps with full written client consent.
</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="How is a fractional GC different from an advocate on a traditional retainer?">
Traditional retainers are typically narrow — a lawyer is engaged for a specific matter or a narrow advisory scope, and the client contact is transactional. A fractional GC is engaged for breadth and context — the lawyer embeds in the client's operating cadence (board meetings, management discussions, HR issues, contract flow), serves as single point of legal contact across functions, and develops deep institutional knowledge. The engagement period is longer-tenure (typically 18+ months) and the monthly retainer is larger.
</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="Can a fractional GC work from outside India while serving Indian clients?">
An Indian-enrolled advocate may serve Indian clients while being physically resident outside India, subject to several considerations. First, maintaining an active Bar Council enrolment and COP in the State where the advocate is on the roll. Second, tax residency implications under the Income Tax Act, 1961 — residence status affects how the income is taxed. Third, data-handling considerations under the DPDP Act, 2023, as storing client personal data outside India may attract cross-border transfer provisions once the relevant sections are notified. Many NRI advocates operate this model successfully with careful tax and compliance planning.
</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="How do you transition from a law firm partnership to a fractional GC practice?">
Plan a 9-12 month transition. Step 1: Define the fractional GC niche and identify 10-15 potential first-year clients from your existing network. Step 2: Negotiate an exit from the firm's partnership with clear handling of existing matters, restrictive covenants (note that post-employment non-competes are generally unenforceable under Section 27 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872) and client-conflict clearance. Step 3: Set up independent practice infrastructure before the transition date. Step 4: Sign first one or two fractional GC clients before leaving. Step 5: Move laterally to independent practice with retainer income already committed. Avoid a hard break that leaves you with zero retainer income — client acquisition is slower than most first-time fractional GCs expect.
</FAQItem>
