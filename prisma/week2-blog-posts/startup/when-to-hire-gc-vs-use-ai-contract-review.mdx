# When to Hire a General Counsel vs Use AI Contract Review: An Honest Framework

The question comes up almost every week on founder group chats and Twitter DMs: *At what point do I actually need to hire a General Counsel, and until then, what can I reasonably do with AI and templates?*

The founders asking it are right to ask. Legal is one of the easiest functions to over-spend on and one of the worst functions to under-invest in. Getting the timing wrong in either direction costs real money — either by burning ₹40 lakh a year on a full-time GC who is reviewing five NDAs a month, or by deferring the hire until an employment dispute or data breach turns into a disclosable risk during a Series B round.

This guide is a no-bullshit framework for Indian startups deciding between three legal operating models — DIY with AI, fractional/outside counsel, and in-house GC — across pre-seed, seed and Series A stages.

<KeyTakeaway>
- Most Indian startups do not need a full-time General Counsel until Series B or later, provided they combine AI contract review with a strong external counsel relationship.
- At pre-seed and seed, the economic case for a GC is almost always negative; fractional legal support with AI-augmented review is structurally more efficient.
- Hybrid models — founder + AI for Tier 3 contracts, external lawyer for Tier 1 matters — consistently outperform all-or-nothing approaches.
- A GC hired too early will spend their time on work a contract intelligence tool handles in thirty seconds.
- A GC hired too late will be buried under a backlog of unreviewed contracts the week they join.
</KeyTakeaway>

## The Three Legal Operating Models

Indian startups have three realistic ways to handle legal work:

1. **DIY + AI** — The founder (or an operations hire) handles routine contracts using templates and AI contract review tools. External counsel is used only for high-stakes matters.
2. **Fractional / Outside Counsel** — A law firm or independent lawyer is engaged on retainer or ad-hoc for all legal matters.
3. **In-House GC** — A full-time, senior lawyer sits inside the company, usually reporting to the CEO or COO.

The answer is almost never "pick one and stick with it." It is "choose the right blend for your stage."

## The Cost Base You Are Actually Comparing

Before answering *when*, it helps to have numbers.

| Model | Typical cost (India, 2026) | What you get |
|---|---|---|
| DIY + AI contract review | ₹0–₹2,00,000/year (tool + templates) | Founder-led review; AI redlines and risk flags; standard templates |
| Boutique firm retainer | ₹50,000–₹2,00,000/month | Partner-level advice on call, plus drafting |
| Top-tier firm (Cyril, Khaitan, AZB, SAM, JSA, Trilegal) | ₹10,000–₹50,000/hour or ₹5,00,000+/month retainer | Full-stack premium support, primarily for fundraising and enterprise diligence |
| Fractional / part-time lawyer | ₹1,00,000–₹3,00,000/month | Three to ten hours a week of senior support |
| In-house GC (Series A to B typical range) | ₹40–₹80 lakh/year (CTC) + ESOPs | Dedicated resource, deep company context |

The cost of a full-time GC is not just cash. It is a senior hire who consumes founder attention, needs tooling (CLM, e-signature, playbooks), and has to be integrated into every workflow that already runs.

## The Three Tiers of Contract Work

The strongest predictor of the right operating model is the mix of contract types the company is handling. Contracts fall into three tiers by risk and complexity:

- **Tier 1 — Company-defining.** Fundraising documents (SHA, SSA, SAFE, convertible note), acquisition-related agreements, strategic partnership contracts, large customer enterprise deals (>₹1 crore annual value), major vendor deals with long lock-ins, employment contracts for C-suite.
- **Tier 2 — Significant risk.** Customer MSAs for mid-market deals, employment contracts for senior hires, vendor contracts with data processing obligations, IP licensing, distribution agreements.
- **Tier 3 — High volume, standardised.** Routine NDAs, freelancer MSAs, standard customer contracts on pre-approved templates, office lease renewals, standard vendor POs.

Tier 1 always needs a human lawyer. Tier 3 almost never does. The middle tier is where AI plus a thoughtful founder gets you 90% of the way.

<Callout variant="info" title="Why 'Just Hire a GC' Misses the Point">
Even at companies with a full in-house legal team, it is not economically sensible to have senior lawyers reviewing routine NDAs. Mature legal teams use contract intelligence tools precisely because routing every Tier 3 contract to a human is waste. The question is never 'AI or lawyer?' — it is 'where does human judgment actually add value?'
</Callout>

## Stage-by-Stage Framework

### Pre-Seed (₹0–₹2 crore raised, 2–10 people)

**Default stack:** Founder-led + AI contract review + ad-hoc boutique firm for incorporation and founders' agreement.

**Why:** The total contract volume is low (20–40 contracts a year). The matters that require real legal depth — incorporation, founders' agreement, initial trademark filings — are one-time events. Paying a lawyer ₹50,000 to draft a reusable NDA template is a fine investment; paying them ₹5,000 to review each individual NDA afterwards is not.

**When to escalate to a lawyer:**

- Incorporation and MoA/AoA drafting.
- First founders' agreement and vesting structure.
- First angel investment documents (SAFE, convertible note, CCPS).
- Any IP dispute or C&D letter.
- Any employment termination that might be contested.
- Any DPDP-related notice or grievance.

**What AI handles well at this stage:**

- NDAs (mutual, unilateral, customer, vendor).
- Standard vendor T&Cs review (SaaS terms, payment gateways, cloud providers).
- Freelancer MSAs and SOWs.
- Standard customer contracts built from a playbook.
- Employment offer letters (for junior and mid hires).

**Estimated annual spend:** ₹1,50,000–₹4,00,000 total (tools + boutique firm + trademark filings).

### Seed (₹2–₹15 crore raised, 10–30 people)

**Default stack:** AI contract review (primary) + retained boutique firm (for Tier 1 and complex Tier 2) + optional part-time fractional lawyer.

**Why:** Contract volume scales up sharply — often 80–150 contracts a year. Enterprise sales conversations start, which means Master Services Agreements, Data Processing Addenda, security questionnaires, and vendor assessments. Employment hiring ramps. A GC is still premature — the role would be ~30% utilised on real legal work and 70% on administrative overflow.

**When to escalate to a lawyer:**

- Series A term sheet and SHA/SSA negotiation.
- First enterprise customer with non-standard redlines.
- Any customer deal with DPDP implications or international data transfer.
- Any acquisition conversation (even informal).
- C-suite employment contracts and ESOP grants to senior hires.
- Any regulatory inquiry.

**What AI handles well at this stage:**

- All Tier 3 contracts.
- First-pass redline of customer-side MSAs and DPAs.
- Standard customer deals negotiated against a pre-approved playbook.
- Ongoing vendor contract renewals.
- Employment contracts for individual contributors.

**The fractional option:** At late seed, many founders retain a fractional lawyer for 5–15 hours a month (₹1–3 lakh/month). This is usually a former GC or senior associate who understands the startup context. They do not replace the top-tier firm for fundraising — they sit in between AI and the big firm, catching what AI cannot and keeping the big firm out of tasks it would be overqualified for.

**Estimated annual spend:** ₹6,00,000–₹15,00,000 total.

### Series A (₹15–₹60 crore raised, 30–80 people)

**Default stack:** AI contract review + fractional GC (full engagement) + top-tier law firm for fundraising, M&A, enterprise.

**Why:** At Series A, the company is signing enterprise customers, has real employment obligations, is in active negotiations with a potential Series B, and has enough vendor relationships that contract admin becomes a meaningful function. Full-time GC economics start to be defensible but are still borderline.

**Signals that you actually need a GC (not just fractional):**

- Enterprise deal cycles where customer procurement routes all contracts through their own legal — multiple deals per month.
- Multi-jurisdictional operations (India + US/Singapore/UK) with ongoing compliance obligations.
- Regulated industry (fintech, healthtech, edtech) with continuous regulatory engagement.
- An acquisition pipeline or an active M&A conversation that will close in 6–12 months.
- Ten or more contracts per week crossing your desk where a human has to make a judgment call.

**Signals you do NOT yet need a GC:**

- Contract volume is high but concentrated in standardised templates.
- Fundraising happens every 18–24 months and is handled by a top-tier firm.
- Regulatory surface is narrow and stable.
- Existing fractional arrangement is responsive and adequate.

**Estimated annual spend with GC:** ₹50,00,000–₹1 crore (salary + benefits + tooling + top-tier firm for big work).

**Estimated annual spend with AI + fractional + top firm:** ₹20,00,000–₹40,00,000 for the same coverage.

## Decision Matrix: The 5 Questions That Decide It

When a founder asks "should I hire a GC now?", five questions usually settle the answer.

1. **Contract volume per month.** <10/month: AI + fractional. 10–30: fractional + top firm. >30: GC justified.
2. **Regulated industry exposure.** None: GC not needed. Moderate (payments, data, healthcare): fractional with sector specialism. Heavy (RBI/IRDAI/FDA-parallel): GC justified.
3. **Enterprise sales intensity.** <5 enterprise deals/quarter: fractional enough. >10 enterprise deals/quarter: GC pays for itself in cycle-time reduction alone.
4. **M&A proximity.** No conversations in 12 months: GC premature. Active diligence: GC reduces execution risk materially.
5. **Funded runway.** <18 months: do not hire a GC. 24+ months: GC can be justified if the other signals point that way.

If 3 of the 5 point toward a GC, it is probably time. If 2 or fewer, stay fractional.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The 'GC as Extra COO' Mistake">
Founders sometimes hire a GC primarily to handle operational leadership that is not actually legal work — vendor management, HR, finance coordination. This is a fast way to pay ₹60 lakh for a law graduate doing ops work. If the real need is operational, hire a COO or a Head of Operations. GCs are expensive for a reason: their value is legal judgment at scale, not general competence.
</Callout>

## What a Good Hybrid Model Looks Like

A well-run Series A startup in India typically runs something like this:

- **AI contract review** as the first pass for every incoming contract (routine DPAs, vendor agreements, standard MSAs). Outputs risk flags, suggested redlines, and a go/no-go recommendation.
- **Fractional GC** (10–25 hours/month) handles the middle layer: custom customer deals, employment terminations, DPDP incidents, IP strategy, and serves as a bridge to top-tier counsel.
- **Top-tier law firm** is engaged for Tier 1 work only: fundraising rounds, major M&A, complex regulatory matters, and litigation.
- **Playbooks** maintained in a shared workspace define what goes to AI, what goes to fractional, and what goes to top-tier. These playbooks are updated quarterly.

The net cost of this model, well-run, is ~40% of the cost of a dedicated in-house legal function and typically covers 95% of a growing startup's needs from pre-seed through Series A.

## The 10% You Should Always Route to a Human

Regardless of stage or budget, the following should never sit with AI alone:

- Any contract with potential liability >₹1 crore.
- Any document that will be signed during a fundraising round.
- Employment terminations, especially senior hires.
- Intellectual property disputes.
- Regulatory notices or show-cause letters.
- Cross-border data transfer arrangements with DPDP implications.
- Acquisition-related documents.

AI is excellent at speed, consistency and flagging known issues. It is not a lawyer. A good operating model respects that line.

## Frequently Asked Questions

<FAQItem question="At what funding stage do most Indian startups hire their first General Counsel?">
Most Indian startups hire their first dedicated General Counsel between Series B and Series C, typically when the company has crossed 100 employees, has multiple enterprise deals in active negotiation each month, and is entering its next major fundraising cycle. A meaningful minority hire a GC at late Series A, particularly in regulated sectors like fintech, healthtech or B2B SaaS with strong enterprise motion. Pre-seed and seed-stage GC hires are rare and usually indicate either a founder legal background (who promotes themselves into the role later) or a very specific regulatory need.
</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="Can an AI contract review tool actually replace a lawyer for Indian law matters?">
No. AI contract review tools replace the first pass, not the final judgment. A well-built AI tool trained on Indian law — ICA 1872, Companies Act 2013, DPDP Act 2023, Income Tax Act — can identify missing clauses, flag non-standard terms, suggest redlines against a playbook, and reduce the human review time on standard contracts by 70–80%. What it cannot do is exercise judgment under genuine uncertainty, appear in court, negotiate a one-off commercial concession with an enterprise customer, or provide legal advice in the statutory sense. The correct framing is: AI + human lawyer > AI alone > human lawyer alone for routine work.
</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="How much does a fractional GC typically cost in India?">
Fractional or part-time GC arrangements in India typically cost between ₹1,00,000 and ₹3,00,000 per month, depending on the seniority of the lawyer, the sector (fintech and healthtech command premium rates), and the hours committed. A typical engagement is 5–15 hours per week, with a defined scope that usually excludes fundraising (which is routed to a top-tier firm). Fractional lawyers are often ex-law firm senior associates or former in-house counsel who have moved to independent practice. The economics are strongly favourable for Series A startups compared to a full-time GC hire.
</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="Should a pre-seed founder ever spend money on a top-tier law firm like Cyril, Khaitan, or AZB?">
Usually not at pre-seed, with one exception: when the angel round is sophisticated enough that the investor counsel is itself a top-tier firm. In that case, having counsel from a peer firm on the founder side ensures a balanced negotiation. Otherwise, boutique firms and experienced independent lawyers provide better economics for pre-seed matters. Top-tier firm rates (₹10,000–₹50,000/hour) are difficult to justify on documents that a boutique firm can draft equally well for a fraction of the cost. Reserve the top-tier relationship for Series A onwards, when complexity and stakes rise materially.
</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="What are the most common legal tasks startups waste money on by over-lawyering?">
The top three are: (1) routine NDAs — most NDAs can be handled with a company-approved template reviewed by AI, not a billable lawyer; (2) standard vendor onboarding — SaaS vendors use industry-standard terms, so exhaustive redlines on a ₹50,000/year tool are rarely worth the legal fees; (3) employment offer letters for individual contributors — founders sometimes send these to a lawyer each time, when a standard template with AI review handles 95% of cases. Conversely, the areas consistently under-lawyered are founders' agreements, IP assignments from contractors, and DPDP-related clauses in customer contracts.
</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="How does hiring a GC affect the cap table and equity pool?">
A senior GC hire at Series A in India typically commands 0.3% to 1.0% of fully diluted equity (usually 0.5%) over a four-year vesting schedule with a one-year cliff. Later-stage GC hires closer to Series C often receive smaller grants (0.1% to 0.3%) but much higher cash compensation. The ESOP cost of a GC hire is real: if the pool is 15%, a 0.75% grant consumes 5% of the pool. Founders should model this before making the hire rather than after. A fractional GC does not typically receive ESOPs — the arrangement is cash-only, which is another factor in the economics.
</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="Is it a red flag for investors if a Series A startup does not have an in-house lawyer?">
Not at all. In fact, the opposite is often true — investors value capital efficiency, and a startup that is handling legal work effectively through a combination of AI tools, fractional counsel, and a top-tier firm for big matters is demonstrating disciplined resource allocation. The red flags investors actually look for are: a missing or poorly drafted founders' agreement, unclear IP assignments, unresolved employment disputes, DPDP gaps, or unsigned customer contracts. None of these are solved by a GC title — they are solved by good process. The GC conversation typically becomes an investor question only at Series B and beyond.
</FAQItem>
