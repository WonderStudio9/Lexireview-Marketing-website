Over the years, I've refined my contract review checklist into five broad categories. Here's the framework:

**1. Parties and Authority** — Are the entities correctly named? Does the signatory have authority? Check board resolutions if needed.

**2. Commercial Terms** — Payment terms, pricing escalation mechanisms, delivery timelines. These sound obvious but are the #1 source of post-signature disputes.

**3. Risk Allocation** — Indemnities, liability caps, insurance requirements, force majeure. In India, Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act governs frustration of contract, so your force majeure clause must be carefully drafted.

**4. Compliance and Regulatory** — Stamp duty (varies by state), GST implications, data protection under the DPDP Act, sector-specific regulations like RERA or RBI guidelines.

**5. Exit Provisions** — Termination triggers, notice periods, surviving clauses, dispute resolution mechanism (arbitration vs. litigation, seat of arbitration).

I use this at two stages: first pass for initial risk assessment, and again after the counterparty's redline. This two-pass approach catches issues that evolve during negotiation.

## Q4: What is the minimum stamp duty to be paid in a rental agreement in Tamil Nadu?
**Quora URL:** https://www.quora.com/What-is-the-minimum-stamp-duty-to-be-paid-in-a-rental-agreement-in-Tamil-Nadu
**Matching Blog Post:** https://lexireview.in/blog/indian-stamp-duty-rates-2025-state-wise-guide

In Tamil Nadu, the stamp duty for a rental agreement depends on the lease duration.

**For leases up to 1 year:** Stamp duty is 1% of the total rent for the entire period. There is no separate charge on the security deposit if it is refundable.

**For leases between 1–3 years:** Stamp duty is 1% of the total rent for the lease period.

**For leases above 3 years (or in perpetuity):** The stamp duty calculation follows a different slab and can go up to 4%, similar to a conveyance deed.

**Registration:** Any lease exceeding 12 months must be compulsorily registered under the Registration Act, 1908. Registration charges are typically 1% of the average annual rent, subject to a cap.

**Important practical tip:** Many landlords in Chennai try to keep leases at exactly 11 months to avoid registration. While this is common practice, repeatedly renewing 11-month leases can still attract scrutiny from revenue authorities.

Always verify current rates on the Tamil Nadu Registration Department portal, as these are subject to periodic revision.

## Q6: What is the difference between MOU and MOA? Are both legally binding?
**Quora URL:** https://www.quora.com/What-is-the-difference-between-MOU-and-MOA-Are-both-of-these-legally-binding
**Matching Blog Post:** https://lexireview.in/blog/mou-vs-moa-difference-legally-binding-india

This is one of the most misunderstood areas in Indian business law, so let me clarify.

An **MOU (Memorandum of Understanding)** records a mutual understanding between parties — typically at an exploratory stage. It outlines intentions, broad terms, and the framework for future negotiations. Most MOUs are *not* legally binding in their entirety, though specific clauses (like confidentiality or exclusivity) can be.

An **MOA (Memorandum of Agreement)** is a step further. It records agreed-upon terms with greater specificity — obligations, timelines, consideration. An MOA is generally treated as a binding contract if it satisfies the essentials under Section 10 of the Indian Contract Act: free consent, lawful consideration, lawful object, and competent parties.

**The critical point:** Indian courts look at substance, not the title. If your "MOU" contains definite terms, consideration, and clear intent to create legal relations, a court can treat it as a binding agreement regardless of what you call it.

My advice: never assume a document is non-binding simply because someone labelled it "MOU."

## Q8: What are the conditions for applicability of RERA?
**Quora URL:** https://www.quora.com/What-are-the-conditions-for-applicability-of-RERA
**Matching Blog Post:** https://lexireview.in/blog/rera-compliance-checklist-builder-buyer-agreements

RERA (Real Estate Regulation and Development Act, 2016) applies when the following conditions are met:

**1. Project Size Threshold:**
- Land area exceeds 500 sq. metres, OR
- Number of apartments to be developed exceeds 8 (including all phases)
- Projects below this threshold are exempt from registration, though buyers can still file complaints

**2. Type of Development:**
- Applies to both residential and commercial projects
- Covers new projects and ongoing projects that had not received completion certificates as of the date RERA came into force in that state

**3. Exclusions:**
- Renovation, repair, or redevelopment that does not involve marketing or new allotment
- Projects where all buildings have received completion certificates
- Land area up to 500 sq. metres with fewer than 9 apartments

**4. State-Level Variations:**
Each state has its own RERA rules. For example, Maharashtra RERA (MahaRERA) has been particularly active, while some states have been slower in implementation.

**Key obligation for builders:** Register the project before advertising or selling any unit. Non-compliance attracts penalties up to 10% of project cost.

## Q10: How do I review a contract like a professional lawyer?
**Quora URL:** https://www.quora.com/How-do-I-review-a-contract-like-a-professional-lawyer
**Matching Blog Post:** https://lexireview.in/blog/contract-review-checklist-50-points-indian-lawyer

I'll share the approach I've seen seasoned lawyers follow — it's more systematic than most people expect.

**Read it twice.** First read: understand the deal structure, parties, and commercial intent. Second read: go clause by clause with a critical eye. Never try to do both in one pass.

**Build a risk map.** For each clause, ask: what's the worst-case scenario if this goes wrong? Rank risks as high, medium, or low. Focus negotiation energy on high-risk items.

**Check the "boring" clauses.** Governing law, jurisdiction, notice provisions, assignment rights, and entire agreement clauses are often overlooked but can determine the outcome of a dispute years later.

**Cross-reference with applicable law.** In India, certain contractual provisions are void regardless of what the parties agree — for instance, penalty clauses (Section 74, Indian Contract Act) and restraint of trade (Section 27).

**Track your comments.** Use a structured format — clause number, issue, risk level, suggested language. This makes negotiations faster and demonstrates professionalism to the counterparty.

## Q11: What legal points should one consider before working as a freelancer while employed in India?
**Quora URL:** https://www.quora.com/What-legal-points-should-one-consider-before-working-as-a-freelancer
**Matching Blog Post:** https://lexireview.in/blog/freelancer-contract-template-india

This question is increasingly relevant given the rise of side projects and moonlighting in India. Here's what to watch out for:

**1. Check your employment agreement.** Most full-time contracts contain exclusivity or moonlighting clauses. Some prohibit any outside work; others require prior written approval. Violating this can be grounds for termination.

**2. Non-compete and non-solicitation.** While broad non-competes during employment are generally upheld in India (unlike post-employment ones, which are void under Section 27 of the Indian Contract Act), non-solicitation clauses restrict you from approaching your employer's clients.

**3. Intellectual property.** If your freelance work overlaps with your employer's domain, IP ownership disputes can arise. Many employment agreements include broad IP assignment clauses covering work done "during the term of employment."

**4. Tax implications.** Freelance income must be declared separately. You'll need to pay advance tax and can claim deductions under Section 44ADA if turnover is below Rs. 75 lakhs.

**5. Get a proper freelancer contract.** Protect yourself — define scope, payment terms, and IP ownership clearly with each client.

## Q12: Who bears the cost of the rent agreement stamp paper?
**Quora URL:** https://www.quora.com/Who-bears-the-cost-of-the-rent-agreement-stamp-paper
**Matching Blog Post:** https://lexireview.in/blog/indian-stamp-duty-rates-2025-state-wise-guide

There's no universal legal rule in India that places the stamp duty burden on a specific party. However, here's how it works in practice:

**Legal position:** Under the Indian Stamp Act, 1899, the person executing the document (i.e., the person who signs it first or who draws it up) is primarily liable for stamp duty. In most rental agreements, both parties execute the document, so technically both are liable.

**Market practice:** In the majority of Indian cities, the **tenant bears the cost** of the stamp paper and registration. This is simply convention — landlords view it as a cost of the tenant securing the property.

**Exceptions exist:**
- In some corporate leases, the landlord bears the cost as part of the deal
- In commercial leases with high stamp duty, it's often split 50-50
- The agreement itself can specify who pays

**Important:** If a rental agreement is insufficiently stamped, it becomes inadmissible as evidence in court unless the deficiency (plus penalty) is paid later. This affects both parties equally, so it's in everyone's interest to stamp it correctly.

**Cross-border data transfers** — if your contracts involve sending personal data outside India, you'll need to comply with the government's eventual notification on permitted jurisdictions.

## Q14: What is the Indian Contract Act Section 56 — force majeure?
**Quora URL:** Search for this question
**Matching Blog Post:** https://lexireview.in/blog/force-majeure-clauses-indian-contracts-guide

This is a common confusion, so let me clarify the distinction.

**Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act** deals with the "doctrine of frustration" — when a contract becomes impossible to perform due to an event beyond the parties' control. If the entire basis of the contract is destroyed, the contract becomes void. The Supreme Court has interpreted this narrowly — mere difficulty, commercial hardship, or increased cost does not amount to frustration.

**Force majeure**, on the other hand, is a **contractual provision** — it's what the parties themselves define as qualifying events (natural disasters, pandemics, government action, war) and the consequences (suspension, termination, extension of time).

**The critical difference:** Section 56 is a statutory remedy that applies even without a force majeure clause, but it sets a very high bar. A well-drafted force majeure clause gives you a contractual remedy with a lower threshold and more predictable outcomes.

**Post-COVID lesson:** Indian courts examined force majeure claims extensively during the pandemic. Contracts with specific, well-drafted force majeure clauses fared far better than those relying solely on Section 56.

## Q15: What makes a non-compete clause void in India?
**Quora URL:** Search for this question
**Matching Blog Post:** https://lexireview.in/blog/employment-agreement-format-india-template

Indian law takes a clear position on this: **post-employment non-compete clauses are void** under Section 27 of the Indian Contract Act, which renders agreements in restraint of trade unenforceable.

Here's the nuance:

**During employment:** Non-compete restrictions are generally valid. Your employer can legitimately restrict you from working with competitors while you are employed with them.

**After employment:** Any clause that prevents you from practising your profession, trade, or business after leaving is void. Unlike the US or UK, Indian law does not recognise "reasonable" post-employment non-competes — even a 6-month restriction in a limited geography has been struck down.

**What employers use instead:**
- **Non-solicitation clauses** — restricting you from poaching clients or employees. These have better enforceability.
- **Garden leave provisions** — paying you to stay home during the notice period.
- **Confidentiality obligations** — these survive termination and are enforceable.

**One exception:** Non-compete clauses in the sale of goodwill of a business (Section 27, Exception 1) are valid if reasonable in scope.

If your employment agreement has a post-employment non-compete, know that it's likely unenforceable.

## Q17: What are RBI outsourcing guidelines for NBFCs?
**Quora URL:** Search for this question
**Matching Blog Post:** https://lexireview.in/blog/rbi-outsourcing-guidelines-contract-compliance

The RBI's outsourcing framework for NBFCs is something many fintech and lending companies underestimate until compliance time. Here are the essentials:

**Core principle:** An NBFC can outsource operational activities but remains fully responsible for the outsourced function. You cannot outsource accountability.

**Key requirements for outsourcing contracts:**

1. **Board-approved policy** — The NBFC must have a documented outsourcing policy approved by its board.
2. **Due diligence on service providers** — Financial health, capability, business continuity planning.
3. **Contractual safeguards** — The agreement must include audit rights, confidentiality obligations, performance standards, and termination provisions.
4. **Data security** — Customer data protection is non-negotiable. The service provider must maintain confidentiality and the NBFC must ensure data is not misused.
5. **No outsourcing of core management functions** — Internal audit, compliance, KYC decisions, and lending decisions cannot be outsourced.
6. **Business continuity** — Contingency plans must exist if the service provider fails.

**Practical impact:** If you're a vendor providing services to an NBFC, expect detailed contractual requirements around audit access, data handling, and SLAs. These are regulatory mandates, not negotiable terms.

## Q18: How does a partnership deed differ from company registration?
**Quora URL:** https://www.quora.com/How-partnership-deed-differs-from-company-registration
**Matching Blog Post:** https://lexireview.in/blog/partnership-deed-format-india-template

These are fundamentally different structures, and the choice affects liability, taxation, and fundraising. Let me break it down.

**Partnership Deed** is a contract between partners governed by the Indian Partnership Act, 1932. It defines profit-sharing ratios, capital contributions, roles, and exit terms. Key characteristics:
- No separate legal entity (partners are personally liable)
- Registration is optional (but unregistered firms cannot sue third parties)
- Minimum 2, maximum 50 partners
- Simpler compliance, lower cost to maintain

**Company Registration** creates a separate legal entity under the Companies Act, 2013 through incorporation with the Registrar of Companies. Key characteristics:
- Limited liability — personal assets are protected
- Mandatory registration and annual compliance (ROC filings, audits)
- Easier to raise external funding
- Perpetual succession — company survives if a director exits

**When to choose which:**
- Early-stage professional practices (CA firms, law firms) → Partnership
- Scalable businesses seeking investment → Private Limited Company
- Small family businesses with trusted partners → Partnership or LLP

An LLP (Limited Liability Partnership) offers a middle ground — partnership flexibility with limited liability.

## Q19: Is NDA required to be signed on stamp paper in India?
**Quora URL:** Search for this question
**Matching Blog Post:** https://lexireview.in/blog/nda-format-india-free-template

Short answer: It depends on the state, but in most cases, **yes, stamp paper is advisable** for an NDA in India.

**Legal position:** An NDA is a contract, and under the Indian Stamp Act, most agreements require stamp duty. The specific value depends on the state where the NDA is executed. In many states, a general agreement attracts a nominal stamp duty — often Rs. 100 to Rs. 500.

**What happens without stamp paper?** The NDA is not automatically void or invalid, but it becomes **inadmissible as evidence** in court. This means if the other party breaches confidentiality, you'll need to pay the deficient stamp duty plus a penalty (often 2–10x the original duty) before the court will even look at the document.

**Practical approach:**
- For high-value NDAs (M&A, joint ventures): always use appropriate stamp paper and consider registration
- For routine business NDAs: Rs. 100–200 stamp paper in most states is sufficient
- For international NDAs with Indian parties: stamp the Indian counterpart's copy at minimum

**E-stamp** is a convenient alternative in states that support it and eliminates the risk of counterfeit stamp paper.
