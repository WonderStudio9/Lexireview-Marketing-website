# Contract Review Checklist: 50 Points Every Indian Lawyer Must Check

> **Import this article using Medium's import tool (medium.com/p/import) with canonical URL: https://lexireview.in/blog/contract-review-checklist-50-points-indian-lawyer**

After analyzing 2,500+ Indian contracts, we identified the 50 points that separate a thorough contract review from a risky rubber stamp. This is the checklist our AI uses internally — and it catches issues that experienced lawyers miss.

---

## How to Use This Checklist

The 50 checkpoints are organized into 10 categories that follow the natural structure of most Indian commercial contracts. For a typical 20-30 page agreement, working through this checklist manually takes approximately 2-3 hours.

The recommended workflow:

1. **Initial scan** — read the contract once for overall structure and commercial intent
2. **Structured checkpoint review** — work through each of the 50 points systematically
3. **Risk matrix compilation** — catalog all findings by severity
4. **Internal stakeholder discussion** — align on acceptable risk levels
5. **Negotiation markup** — prepare redlines for the counterparty

---

## A. Parties and Recitals (Points 1-5)

**1. Legal name verification** — Confirm the exact registered name of each party against MCA records or the relevant registrar. Incorrect names can create enforcement problems.

**2. Signatory authority** — Verify that each signatory has actual authority to bind the entity. Check board resolutions, powers of attorney, or partnership deeds as applicable.

**3. Recital accuracy** — Ensure background clauses accurately describe the transaction context. Recitals are used as interpretive aids by Indian courts and inaccurate recitals can undermine your position in disputes.

**4. Registered address and communication details** — Confirm addresses match official records. These determine where notices are validly served under the contract.

**5. Capacity to contract** — Verify each party satisfies the competency requirements under the Indian Contract Act 1872: major, of sound mind, and not disqualified by law.

---

## B. Definitions (Points 6-10)

**6. Comprehensive defined terms** — Every capitalized term used in the contract must have a corresponding definition. Undefined terms create ambiguity that benefits the drafter's counterparty.

**7. Internal consistency** — Defined terms must be used consistently throughout. A term defined one way in the definitions section but used differently in the operative clauses creates exploitable gaps.

**8. No circular definitions** — Term A should not be defined by reference to Term B, which in turn references Term A. This is more common than you would expect.

**9. Statutory alignment** — Where the contract uses terms that have specific statutory meanings (e.g., "wages" under the Code on Wages, "personal data" under the DPDP Act), ensure the contractual definition aligns with or deliberately departs from the statutory definition.

**10. Scope boundaries** — Key commercial terms like "Services," "Deliverables," and "Confidential Information" should be defined with enough specificity to prevent scope creep disputes.

---

## C. Obligations and Rights (Points 11-18)

**11. Mutual vs. unilateral obligations** — Identify which obligations bind both parties and which bind only one. Imbalanced obligations are a negotiation priority.

**12. Performance standards** — Obligations should include measurable performance criteria, not vague language like "reasonable efforts" without further definition.

**13. Timelines and milestones** — Every performance obligation should have a clear deadline or milestone schedule. Open-ended obligations are practically unenforceable.

**14. Conditions precedent** — Identify any conditions that must be satisfied before obligations activate. Ensure these are within the responsible party's control.

**15. Representations and warranties** — Distinguish between representations (statements of current fact) and warranties (promises about future states). Each carries different legal consequences for breach.

**16. Efforts standards** — If the contract uses "best efforts," "reasonable efforts," or "commercially reasonable efforts," understand these carry different legal weight. Define the standard clearly.

**17. Reporting and notification obligations** — Check for obligations to report, notify, or seek approval. Missing a notification requirement can constitute a material breach.

**18. Change management** — Ensure there is a clear process for modifying scope, timelines, or deliverables during the contract term. Absence of a change mechanism leads to disputes.

---

## D. Payment and Consideration (Points 19-23)

**19. Payment amounts and structure** — Verify all amounts are clearly stated, including the basis for any variable components. Ambiguity in payment terms is the most common source of commercial disputes.

**20. Payment schedule and triggers** — Define when payments are due and what triggers each payment. Link payments to verifiable milestones where possible.

**21. Late payment consequences** — Include interest rates for delayed payment. In the absence of a contractual rate, courts apply variable standards that may not align with your commercial expectations.

**22. GST treatment** — Specify whether amounts are inclusive or exclusive of GST, which party bears the tax liability, and the invoicing format requirements for input tax credit eligibility.

**23. Audit rights** — Include the right to audit payment calculations, especially in revenue-sharing, royalty, or variable-fee arrangements. Without contractual audit rights, you have limited recourse to verify payments.

---

## E. Intellectual Property and Confidentiality (Points 24-28)

**24. IP ownership** — Explicitly state who owns intellectual property created during the contract. Under Indian law, absent a written assignment, the creator generally retains ownership.

**25. Licensing scope** — If IP is licensed rather than assigned, define the scope precisely: exclusive or non-exclusive, territory, duration, sublicensing rights, and permitted uses.

**26. Background IP carve-outs** — Protect pre-existing IP that each party brings to the relationship. Without clear carve-outs, background IP can become entangled with newly created IP.

**27. Confidentiality protections** — Ensure confidentiality obligations cover the right information, with appropriate exclusions, reasonable duration, and permitted disclosures. Cross-reference the DPDP Act if personal data is involved.

**28. Data protection obligations** — Under the DPDP Act 2023, contracts involving personal data must address fiduciary obligations, processing limitations, breach notification, and data principal rights.

---

## F. Liability and Indemnity (Points 29-33)

> **High-Risk Area:** Liability and indemnity provisions represent some of the highest-risk elements in any contract. Review these with particular scrutiny.

**29. Liability caps** — Check whether aggregate liability is capped and at what level. Uncapped liability in a commercial contract is a significant risk. Common benchmarks: 1x-2x annual contract value.

**30. Consequential damages exclusion** — Verify whether indirect, consequential, or special damages are excluded. Note any carve-outs (IP infringement, confidentiality breach, and willful misconduct are commonly excluded from exclusion clauses).

**31. Indemnity scope** — Define clearly what losses trigger indemnification: third-party claims, regulatory penalties, IP infringement, data breaches. Avoid unlimited indemnity obligations.

**32. Indemnity procedures** — Specify notice requirements, the right to control defense, settlement authority, and cooperation obligations. Procedural failures can defeat indemnity claims.

**33. Insurance requirements** — Where risks are significant, require appropriate insurance coverage (professional liability, cyber liability, employer's liability) with minimum coverage amounts and certificate requirements.

---

## G. Termination (Points 34-37)

**34. Termination triggers** — Identify all events giving rise to termination rights: material breach, insolvency, change of control, regulatory change, prolonged force majeure. Ensure material breach is defined.

**35. Convenience termination** — Check whether either party has the right to terminate without cause. If so, verify the notice period is commercially reasonable and the financial consequences are acceptable.

**36. Notice and cure periods** — Ensure the breaching party receives written notice and a reasonable opportunity to cure before termination takes effect. Standard cure periods range from 15-30 days.

**37. Post-termination obligations** — Define what happens after termination: data return or destruction, transition assistance, survival of confidentiality and indemnity obligations, final payments, and wind-down procedures.

---

## H. Dispute Resolution (Points 38-41)

**38. Governing law** — Specify which jurisdiction's law governs the contract. This affects interpretation, available remedies, and procedural requirements.

**39. Jurisdiction** — Designate courts with exclusive jurisdiction for disputes. Consider whether the chosen jurisdiction is convenient for enforcement.

**40. Arbitration completeness** — If arbitration is chosen, specify the seat, institutional rules (or ad hoc procedure), number of arbitrators, language, and qualification requirements. Incomplete arbitration clauses are a frequent source of preliminary disputes.

**41. Escalation mechanisms** — Consider a multi-tier dispute resolution process: negotiation between designated executives, followed by mediation, then arbitration or litigation. This reduces legal costs for disputes that can be resolved commercially.

---

## I. Regulatory Compliance (Points 42-46)

**42. DPDP Act 2023 compliance** — If the contract involves personal data processing, verify all data protection provisions: fiduciary obligations, processing limitations, consent mechanisms, breach notification, and cross-border transfer restrictions.

**43. GST compliance** — Ensure the contract facilitates proper GST invoicing, input tax credit claims, and place-of-supply determinations. Incorrect GST treatment can create unexpected tax liabilities.

**44. Stamp duty adequacy** — Verify the contract is executed on stamp paper of appropriate value for the relevant state. Inadequate stamping renders the document inadmissible as evidence.

**45. Sector-specific regulations** — Check compliance with applicable sector regulators: RBI for financial services, SEBI for listed entities, TRAI for telecom, RERA for real estate, and others as applicable.

**46. Anti-corruption provisions** — Include representations and warranties regarding compliance with the Prevention of Corruption Act and any applicable foreign anti-corruption laws (FCPA, UK Bribery Act) if the counterparty has foreign connections.

---

## J. Boilerplate and Execution (Points 47-50)

**47. Force majeure** — Review the force majeure clause for comprehensive coverage, notice requirements, mitigation obligations, and termination rights for prolonged events. Post-COVID, this clause receives significantly more scrutiny.

**48. Assignment restrictions** — Verify whether assignment or novation requires consent, and whether change-of-control triggers any rights. Unrestricted assignment can result in your contract being performed by an unknown entity.

**49. Entire agreement clause** — Confirm the contract supersedes all prior discussions, and that any side letters, schedules, or annexures are properly incorporated by reference.

**50. Execution formalities** — Verify proper execution: authorized signatories, appropriate stamp duty, witness requirements (where applicable), and compliance with any regulatory filing obligations.

## Prioritizing Your Review

Not all 50 points carry equal risk. Based on analysis of thousands of Indian contracts, the highest-risk areas that deserve the most scrutiny are:

1. **Liability and indemnity provisions** (Points 29-33)
2. **Termination clauses** (Points 34-37)
3. **Payment terms** (Points 19-23)
4. **Dispute resolution** (Points 38-41)
5. **Regulatory compliance** (Points 42-46)

Start with these sections if time is limited, and work outward from there.

Working through 50 checkpoints on a 20-30 page contract takes 2-3 hours of focused legal attention. For organizations processing dozens of contracts monthly, this creates a significant resource constraint.

AI-powered contract review can run all 50 checkpoints simultaneously, flagging risks and missing clauses in seconds rather than hours.
