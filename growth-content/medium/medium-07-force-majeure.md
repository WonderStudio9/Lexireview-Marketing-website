# Force Majeure Clauses in Indian Contracts: Legal Guide

> **Import this article using Medium's import tool (medium.com/p/import) with canonical URL: https://lexireview.in/blog/force-majeure-clauses-indian-contracts-guide**

When COVID-19 hit, Indian courts were flooded with force majeure claims. Most failed --- not because the pandemic was not unprecedented, but because the contract clauses were poorly drafted. Section 56 of the ICA sets a high bar.

Force majeure remains one of the most misunderstood concepts in Indian contract law. Unlike many common law jurisdictions, India has no standalone statute defining force majeure. Instead, the doctrine operates through two distinct legal channels --- express contractual clauses and Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 --- and conflating the two is a mistake that courts penalize regularly.

---

## The Dual Framework: Contractual Clauses vs. Section 56

This is the foundational distinction that every Indian contract drafter must understand.

**Express Contractual Clauses** are provisions negotiated and included in the contract itself. They define what events qualify as force majeure, what obligations are affected, and what remedies are available. These clauses allow graduated remedies --- suspension, extension of timelines, renegotiation, or termination.

**Section 56 of the ICA** is the statutory doctrine of frustration. It applies when no express clause exists or when the clause does not cover the specific event. Critically, frustration is an all-or-nothing outcome: it renders the contract void from the point of the frustrating event.

| Aspect | Force Majeure Clause | Section 56 (Frustration) |
|---|---|---|
| Source | Contractual provision | Statutory doctrine |
| Threshold | As defined in the clause | Impossibility of performance |
| Consequences | Graduated remedies | Contract becomes void |
| Burden of Proof | As specified in clause | On the party claiming frustration |
| Commercial Hardship | Can be included | Explicitly excluded |

> **Key Takeaway:** If your contract has a well-drafted force majeure clause, courts will interpret it as a complete code for dealing with the specified events. If the clause is silent or absent, Section 56 is the fallback --- but its threshold is significantly higher.

## Landmark Supreme Court Precedents

Indian courts have built a robust body of force majeure jurisprudence through four pivotal cases:

**Satyabrata Ghose v. Mugneeram Bangur (1954):** The Supreme Court established that Section 56 applies when the very foundation of the contract is undermined. Temporary inconvenience or difficulty does not qualify. The performance must become fundamentally different from what was contemplated.

**Energy Watchdog v. CERC (2017):** This landmark ruling clarified that where a contract contains an express force majeure clause, Section 56 does not apply. The clause becomes the exhaustive framework. The court also confirmed that economic hardship and price fluctuations do not constitute frustration.

**Naihati Jute Mills v. Khyaliram Jagannath (1968):** Reinforced that the doctrine of frustration cannot be invoked merely because performance has become more expensive or burdensome. The obligation must become truly impossible, not just commercially unfavorable.

**Alopi Parshad v. Union of India (1960):** The Supreme Court held that a contract is not frustrated merely because circumstances make it more onerous. The parties are bound by their obligations even if the economic balance shifts dramatically.

## COVID-Era Judicial Interpretations

The pandemic tested force majeure clauses across Indian courts with mixed results. The key takeaways from COVID-era litigation:

- Courts accepted government-imposed lockdowns as force majeure **only where** the clause expressly covered epidemics, pandemics, or government orders
- Contracts with generic language like "acts of God" faced inconsistent outcomes
- Several High Courts held that economic downturn caused by COVID was insufficient --- the lockdown order itself had to prevent performance
- Payment obligations were generally not excused unless the clause explicitly included them

> **Post-COVID Drafting Imperative:** Every force majeure clause drafted after 2020 should explicitly include "pandemic," "epidemic," "government-ordered shutdown," and "public health emergency" as enumerated events. Generic catch-all language is no longer sufficient.

## Seven Essential Elements of an Enforceable Clause

Based on judicial precedent, an Indian force majeure clause must include:

1. **Enumerated Events:** A specific list of qualifying events (natural disasters, epidemics, government actions, war, sanctions, cyber attacks) followed by a carefully worded catch-all provision
2. **Causation Standard:** Clear language establishing that the event must "prevent, hinder, or delay" performance --- not merely make it inconvenient
3. **Notice Requirements:** Mandatory written notice within a specified timeframe (7--14 days is the recommended standard) with details of the event, its impact, and expected duration
4. **Mitigation Obligations:** A duty on the affected party to take reasonable steps to minimize the impact of the force majeure event
5. **Duration and Termination Triggers:** A maximum suspension period (typically 90--180 days) after which either party may terminate
6. **Obligations Affected:** Explicit identification of which obligations are suspended and which survive (particularly payment obligations)
7. **Documentation Requirements:** Evidence standards the affected party must meet to substantiate the claim

## Five Critical Drafting Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Relying on Generic Catch-All Language Alone**
A clause that says only "any event beyond reasonable control" without enumerated examples invites litigation. Courts prefer specific lists supplemented by a catch-all, not a catch-all standing alone.

**2. Failing to Exclude Payment Obligations Explicitly**
Unless your clause expressly states that payment obligations are excused during a force majeure event, courts will likely hold that the obligation to pay survives. This was a recurring issue in COVID-era disputes.

**3. Omitting Notice Timeframes**
Without a specified notice period, disputes arise about whether notice was timely. A 7--14 day written notice requirement with specific content obligations provides certainty for both parties.

**4. Ignoring the Distinction Between Suspension and Termination**
Many clauses leap straight to termination rights without providing for an interim suspension period. Courts view graduated remedies more favorably --- suspension first, then renegotiation, then termination if the event persists.

**5. Using Identical Clauses Across All Contract Types**
A force majeure clause in a construction contract should look different from one in a software services agreement. The enumerated events, affected obligations, and remedy structure should reflect the specific commercial context.

## Force Majeure vs. Material Adverse Change (MAC) Clauses

A related but distinct concept is the Material Adverse Change clause, commonly found in M&A and financing agreements. While force majeure addresses specific external events preventing performance, MAC clauses address broader changes in circumstances affecting the value proposition of the deal.

The two clauses serve different purposes and should not be treated as interchangeable. A well-drafted commercial agreement may need both --- force majeure for performance obligations and MAC for conditions precedent or closing conditions.

## Sector-Specific Considerations

**Construction and Infrastructure:** Force majeure clauses in EPC contracts should address monsoon seasons, regulatory approvals, and land acquisition delays --- events that are common but can genuinely prevent performance.

**Technology and SaaS:** Cloud outages, cybersecurity incidents, and data localization orders are increasingly relevant force majeure events for technology contracts.

**Financial Services:** RBI and SEBI regulatory actions, sanctions compliance, and payment system outages need specific treatment in BFSI contracts.

**Supply Chain Agreements:** Shipping disruptions, port closures, and export/import restrictions require detailed force majeure provisions given recent global supply chain volatility.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does Indian law recognize force majeure as a statutory concept?

No. India has no standalone force majeure statute. The concept operates either through express contractual provisions or through Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act (doctrine of frustration). If your contract does not contain a force majeure clause, Section 56 is the only recourse --- and its threshold is much higher.

### Can COVID-19 be invoked as force majeure in 2025?

For new contracts, COVID itself is no longer an "unforeseeable" event. It is now a known risk that parties are expected to plan for. However, specific government-imposed restrictions in response to future health emergencies could qualify if the clause covers such events.

### Does force majeure excuse payment obligations?

Generally, no. Indian courts have consistently held that the obligation to pay money is rarely impossible to perform. Unless your force majeure clause expressly includes payment obligations among the affected duties, expect courts to hold that payments remain due.

### What is the recommended notice period?

Seven to fourteen days from the occurrence of the event, with written notice specifying the event, its impact on performance, estimated duration, and mitigation steps being taken. Shorter periods are acceptable for events requiring immediate action.

### Can economic hardship qualify as force majeure?

No. The Supreme Court has repeatedly held that increased costs, currency fluctuations, market downturns, and other forms of economic hardship do not constitute force majeure or frustration under Section 56. The obligation must become impossible, not merely more expensive.

## Conclusion

Force majeure drafting in India requires precision. The dual framework of contractual clauses and Section 56 means that vague or poorly structured provisions leave parties exposed to outcomes they did not anticipate. Post-COVID jurisprudence has raised the bar for both drafting standards and evidentiary requirements.

The most effective approach is a clause that is specific in its enumerated events, clear in its notice and mitigation requirements, graduated in its remedies, and tailored to the commercial context of the specific agreement.
